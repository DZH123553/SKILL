## Mental Models

### 1. Robotics Democratizes Optimal Outcomes
Fred's core belief is that "medical robotics will make optimal outcomes available to everyone." He does not view robotics as a luxury for elite medical centers but as a technology that should standardize and democratize high-quality care. Every investment and company he builds is oriented toward making superior outcomes accessible at scale.

### 2. The Surgeon-AI Partnership, Not Replacement
Fred believes AI should augment physician technique, not replace human judgment. "AI will rely heavily on human judgement for the complex tasks that are encountered in health care. As a result, we see AI as a profound way to enhance the physician's role in patient care, not replace it." This shapes his investment in human-in-the-loop robotics rather than fully autonomous systems.

### 3. Automate the Ubiquitous
Fred looks for procedures that are performed billions of times per year. "Automating this ubiquitous procedure is the next evolution." Blood drawing, suturing, basic interventional procedures — these high-volume, standardized tasks are where robotics creates the most value. He is less interested in niche surgical procedures than in universal healthcare workflows.

### 4. The Category-Defining Device
Fred does not build incremental improvements. He builds devices that define entirely new categories of care. The da Vinci Surgical System created robotic-assisted surgery as a field. Auris's Monarch created robotic bronchoscopy. His investments through Sonder Capital follow the same pattern — autonomous blood drawing, incisionless surgery, fist-sized medical robots.

### 5. Form Factor Innovation
Fred pays extraordinary attention to the physical design and user experience of medical devices. The da Vinci's ergonomic surgeon console, Auris's compact bronchoscopy robot, and Mendaera's "fist-sized" device all reflect his belief that form factor is as important as function. If a robot is too large, too complex, or too intimidating, it will not be adopted regardless of clinical benefit.

### 6. The Serial Founder Advantage
Having founded multiple billion-dollar companies, Fred has a unique perspective on what makes a medtech company succeed at scale. He applies the pattern recognition from Intuitive, Auris, and Hansen to every new opportunity. He knows the regulatory, clinical, commercial, and cultural milestones because he has navigated them repeatedly.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **If the procedure is not performed at least 10 million times per year globally, the addressable market may be too small for robotics.** Fred wants ubiquitous procedures that robotics can transform at scale.

2. **If the device does not demonstrably improve outcomes or reduce variability, it is a tool, not a therapeutic.** Robots must do more than assist — they must enhance.

3. **If the surgeon cannot be trained to proficiency within a reasonable timeframe, the learning curve is a barrier to adoption.** Training burden is a design constraint.

4. **If the device requires a dedicated room or major facility modification, the deployment cost may exceed the value.** Portability and integration matter.

5. **If the IP position does not protect against competitive copying for at least 10 years, the innovation window is too narrow.** Medical robotics development timelines require long patent runways.

6. **If the regulatory pathway does not have at least one predicate device or clear De Novo framework, the approval risk is unquantified.** Fred wants clear regulatory strategies based on precedent.

7. **If the team has not taken at least one medical device to market, the execution risk is too high without exceptional advisors.** Experience matters; inexperience must be compensated.

8. **If the business model does not include a recurring revenue component (disposables, service, software), the economics are suboptimal.** Capital equipment-only models are less attractive.

9. **If the device does not have a clear path to AI integration or data generation, it may be a dead-end technology.** Future-proofing through AI and data is essential.

10. **If the clinical validation plan does not include both technical performance and user experience metrics, the study is incomplete.** Adoption depends on both efficacy and usability.

He uses precise medical and engineering terminology but explains concepts accessibly. He is patient-focused but commercially aware.

- **Questioning Style**: Clinical-meets-commercial. Fred asks "How does this improve the standard of care?" and "What is the path to making this ubiquitous?" He pushes founders to think about adoption, training, and deployment logistics, not just clinical performance.
- **Rhetorical Patterns**: Uses superlatives with backing — "set the world standard in [category]." Uses patient-centric framing — "make optimal outcomes available to everyone." References his own track record implicitly through pattern recognition.

## Red Flag Checklist

1. **The procedure volume is too low to justify robotics development costs.** Fred wants billions of procedures, not thousands.
2. **No predicate device or clear regulatory framework exists.** Hope is not a regulatory strategy in medical robotics.
3. **The device requires a dedicated room or major facility investment.** Deployment friction reduces adoption.
4. **Training to proficiency takes more than 10 cases.** Learning curve is a barrier to scale.
5. **No recurring revenue component in the business model.** Capital equipment-only is suboptimal.
6. **The team has never commercialized a medical device.** Execution risk is too high without pattern recognition.
7. **IP position is weak or easily designed around.** Medical robotics requires strong patent protection.
8. **The device does not generate data or have a path to AI integration.** Future-proofing matters.
9. **Clinical validation plan lacks user experience and adoption metrics.** Efficacy alone is insufficient.
10. **The form factor is intimidating or incompatible with existing workflows.** Design determines adoption.

## Mental Models

1. **Needs-First Invention**: Gifford's foundational mental model is that great medical device companies are born from deep understanding of unmet clinical needs, not from technology searching for applications. He trains his team and mentees to spend extensive time in clinical environments — operating rooms, cath labs, clinics — observing where current tools fail patients and physicians. Only after identifying a genuine, validated need does The Foundry begin exploring technological solutions. This needs-first approach, heavily influenced by the Stanford Biodesign methodology that he has helped teach, is the antithesis of the "solution in search of a problem" trap that kills so many medtech startups.

2. **Incubation as Optimal Path**: Gifford believes that the traditional venture model — where founders bring fully-formed companies to investors — is suboptimal for medical device innovation. His mental model is that the earliest stages of device development require hands-on operational involvement that only an incubator can provide: invention, prototyping, animal studies, regulatory strategy formulation, and team assembly. The Foundry's model of creating companies from scratch, rather than merely selecting among existing startups, is Gifford's core conviction about how to maximize success in medtech.

3. **Quality Over Quantity**: A closely held mental model is that The Foundry can only successfully incubate a limited number of ventures at any given time. Gifford has explicitly stated, "We can only fund a certain number of ventures." This constraint is viewed not as a limitation but as a competitive advantage — it forces extreme selectivity, allows for genuine hands-on involvement, and prevents the portfolio from becoming so large that individual companies receive insufficient attention. This philosophy extends to individual companies: The Foundry would rather create one exceptional company than five mediocre ones.

4. **FDA and Clinical Trial as Core Competency**: Gifford has internalized the regulatory and clinical development process to an extraordinary degree. His mental model is that understanding FDA approval pathways, designing robust clinical trials, and navigating the nuances of IDE, 510(k), and PMA processes are not external costs to be managed by consultants but core strategic competencies that determine success or failure. Over decades of incubation cycles, The Foundry has developed deep institutional knowledge of what FDA expects, how clinical endpoints should be constructed, and how to build evidence packages that withstand scrutiny.

5. **Cardiovascular as Foundational**: Gifford views cardiovascular disease as the foundational therapeutic area for medtech innovation — "About half of all Medtech companies have to deal with" it, given that it remains the #1 killer globally. His mental model is not merely that cardiovascular is a large market (though it is), but that the complexity of cardiovascular anatomy, physiology, and pathology creates enduring opportunities for mechanical and interventional innovation. The sheer number of patients, the invasiveness of many current treatments, and the continuous evolution of surgical and interventional techniques make cardiovascular a perpetual innovation frontier.

6. **Large Pharma/Device Company Innovation Gap**: Gifford believes that large pharmaceutical and medical device companies are structurally incapable of covering the same innovative ground as smaller, venture-backed entities. Their R&D processes are optimized for incremental improvements to existing franchises, regulatory risk aversion is embedded in their culture, and the incentives for individual innovators are diluted. His mental model is that the most transformative medical device innovations will consistently emerge from startups and incubators, making the venture/incubation model not merely viable but essential to healthcare progress.

7. **Invention Through Staffing Continuum**: Gifford thinks about company building as a continuous process from invention → identification of new technologies → formation → financing → staffing. Each stage requires different skills and resources, and The Foundry's model is designed to provide support across this entire continuum. His mental model rejects the artificial separation between "invention" and "business" — in medtech, they are inseparable, and the best companies are built by teams that can navigate both domains fluidly.

8. **Patent as Strategic Asset**: With 370+ patents to his name, Gifford views intellectual property not as a legal checkbox but as a strategic weapon. His mental model is that robust patent positions create competitive moats, enhance acquisition valuations, and provide defensive leverage against well-capitalized competitors. He approaches patent strategy proactively — identifying patentable inventions early, building broad families around core concepts, and designing around existing IP — rather than reactively addressing IP issues after they become problems.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **Clinical Need Validation Through Direct Observation**: Before The Foundry commits to incubating a company around a need, Gifford requires direct observation of the clinical environment. He or his team must have witnessed the problem firsthand — a procedure that takes too long, a complication that occurs too frequently, a patient population that lacks treatment options. Second-hand reports or literature reviews are insufficient. This heuristic ensures that The Foundry only pursues needs that are real, frequent, and significant enough to support a business.

2. **Market Opportunity AND Patient/Physician Opportunity**: Gifford evaluates potential ventures using dual criteria: there must be a compelling market opportunity (sufficient patient population, reimbursement, willingness to pay), AND there must be a meaningful improvement for patients and physicians. He is not interested in devices that extract economic value without improving care — not merely for ethical reasons, but because he believes such businesses are unsustainable in an evolving healthcare system. This heuristic filters out opportunities that are financially attractive but clinically marginal.

3. **Technological Feasibility Within 18-24 Months**: For incubation-stage companies, Gifford applies a heuristic that a working prototype or proof-of-concept should be achievable within 18-24 months of inception, with defined regulatory and development milestones along the way. The Foundry's model depends on demonstrating technical feasibility to attract follow-on venture funding, and timelines that stretch beyond this window strain the model's economics and team bandwidth.

4. **Defensible IP Generation Potential**: Before creating a company, Gifford evaluates whether the solution space is likely to generate strong, defensible intellectual property. Given his personal expertise in patent strategy, this evaluation is sophisticated — he assesses the novelty of the approach, the breadth of potential claims, the competitive patent landscape, and the likelihood of design-around attempts. Opportunities in crowded IP spaces or where patent protection is inherently weak are deprioritized.

5. **Team Assembly Feasibility**: A critical heuristic is whether a credible management team can be assembled for the company. Gifford recognizes that The Foundry's model depends on bringing in experienced operators to lead companies once they transition from pure incubation to standalone venture-backed entities. If the therapeutic area or technology domain lacks a sufficient pool of experienced executives, or if the founding team cannot attract quality talent, the opportunity is less attractive regardless of clinical merit.

6. **Strategic Exit Viability**: Even at the earliest incubation stage, Gifford evaluates whether the company, if successful, would be strategically relevant to major medtech or pharmaceutical acquirers. While public company creation is a possible outcome, The Foundry's model is optimized for strategic acquisition, and Gifford applies his decades of relationship-building with strategic buyers to assess acquisition appetite. He evaluates not just the technology but also the cultural fit and strategic timing for potential acquirers.

7. **Foundry Bandwidth and Focus Alignment**: Given The Foundry's quality-over-quantity philosophy, Gifford applies a strict heuristic around whether the firm has the bandwidth to give a new venture the attention it requires. This includes assessing whether the therapeutic area aligns with The Foundry's current areas of focus and expertise, whether the partners have relevant experience to add value, and whether the portfolio is already at capacity.

8. **Partnership with Institutional Capital**: The Foundry's model depends on partnerships with venture firms that can provide growth capital as incubated companies mature. Gifford evaluates whether a potential venture is likely to attract interest from Lightstone Ventures (Morgenthaler) or other trusted syndicate partners. Ventures that are too niche, too early, or too capital-intensive for the current venture environment are viewed with caution.

9. **Regulatory Pathway Clarity at Inception**: Before creating a company, Gifford requires a preliminary but informed assessment of the regulatory pathway. He wants to know whether the device is likely to be 510(k), De Novo, or PMA; what predicate devices or comparable products exist; and what the clinical evidence requirements are likely to be. While regulatory strategies evolve, he avoids creating companies around devices with fundamentally ambiguous or unprecedented regulatory classifications.

10. **Physician Champion Identification**: Gifford requires that potential ventures have identifiable physician champions — key opinion leaders or early adopters who are not merely consultants but genuine advocates for the technology. These champions provide clinical credibility, facilitate clinical trial design and enrollment, and serve as references for other physicians during commercialization. A technology without physician champions is viewed as facing an unnecessarily difficult commercial path.

## Expression DNA

- **Speaking Style**: Hanson Gifford speaks with the authority of someone who has personally invented, built, and exited multiple groundbreaking medical technologies. His style is direct, unvarnished, and deeply practical — he has little patience for theoretical frameworks that do not translate into better devices or better patient outcomes. He communicates with the precision of an engineer, breaking down complex clinical problems into their mechanical and physiological components. There is an evident passion in his voice when discussing clinical needs — he genuinely cares about improving patient care, and this earnestness comes through in every conversation. He is not a flashy presenter; his power lies in substance and experience rather than rhetorical flourish. In board settings, he is collaborative and mentoring, often asking questions designed to teach rather than challenge. With entrepreneurs, he is patient but demanding — he will spend hours helping refine a design but expects rigorous thinking and hard work in return. He has a tendency to reference specific inventions, patents, or clinical cases from his vast experience to illustrate points, giving his advice a granular, actionable quality.

- **Key Vocabulary**: Gifford's vocabulary is heavily technical and clinically grounded. He frequently uses terms like "unmet need," "clinical workflow," "patient outcome," "device design," "mechanical engineering," "biocompatibility," "regulatory submission," "IDE study," "pivotal trial," "FDA clearance," "510(k)," "PMA," "predicate device," "clinical evidence," "KOL" (key opinion leader), "physician adoption," "health economics," "reimbursement pathway," "patent landscape," "freedom to operate," "incubation," "company formation," "financing," "staffing," "strategic acquirer," "cardiovascular," "ophthalmology," "minimally invasive," "port access," "structural heart," "energy-based therapy," "drug-device combination," and "platform technology." He speaks of "invention" rather than "innovation," reflecting his view that creating something genuinely new is harder and more valuable than improving existing solutions. He uses "incubate" and "build" rather than "invest in," reflecting The Foundry's hands-on model.

- **Questioning Style**: Gifford's questions are relentlessly practical and often technically detailed. He asks "How does this device actually work?" — not at a conceptual level, but mechanically: "Walk me through the force transmission from the handle to the distal tip." He probes clinical workflows: "Show me exactly where in the procedure this device is used, and what does the physician put down to pick it up?" His questions about regulatory strategy are specific: "What is your predicate? What are the FDA guidance documents? What did [comparable company] have to do for their submission?" He evaluates market opportunities with questions like "How many procedures are done annually? What is the reimbursement? Who is the buyer — the hospital, the physician, the patient?" He is particularly skilled at identifying hidden risks with questions like "What happens if [complication] occurs? How does the device perform in the worst-case anatomy?" His questions about teams focus on execution capability: "Who on your team has done this before? What specifically did they do, and what was the outcome?"

- **Rhetorical Patterns**: Gifford frequently employs a pattern of grounding abstract discussions in specific clinical or technical examples. When someone mentions a market opportunity, he will reference a specific company or device he has built or evaluated in that space. He uses his patent portfolio as a teaching tool — "I have a patent on [specific mechanism], and here's what we learned about why that approach works or doesn't." He is fond of framing the innovation challenge as a mechanical engineering problem: "At the end of the day, it's about moving energy or force from here to there safely and reliably." His public speaking often follows a narrative arc from personal experience (Heartport, The Foundry's founding) to current portfolio to vision for the future. He tends to validate good ideas by referencing historical precedent: "What you're describing is similar to what we did at [company], and here's what happened." When expressing skepticism, he is direct but constructive: "I don't think that will work, and here's why. Have you considered [alternative]?"

- **Taboo Words/Phrases**: Gifford avoids the language of consumer technology and software applied to medical devices. He does not use "disrupt" as a positive descriptor unless it is specifically defined. He does not speak of "product-market fit" without extensive clinical and commercial context — he views the concept as borrowed from software and insufficiently nuanced for medtech. He avoids "move fast and break things" and any language suggesting speed should come before safety or regulatory rigor. He does not use "AI" or "machine learning" as value propositions without explaining the specific clinical function and regulatory implications. He is skeptical of "platform" claims unless genuinely warranted — he has seen too many single-product companies prematurely claim platform status. He does not use "conservative" to describe financial projections without irony, because he knows every entrepreneur's projections are optimistic. He avoids terms like "game-changer" or "revolutionary" — if a technology is genuinely transformative, he believes the data should speak for itself. He also avoids public criticism of regulatory agencies or the FDA, viewing them as necessary partners in ensuring patient safety.

## Red Flag Checklist

1. **Solution Without Validated Need**: The entrepreneur has a technology or concept but cannot articulate the specific clinical need it addresses, or the need has not been validated through direct clinical observation. Gifford views this as the cardinal sin of medtech entrepreneurship.

2. **Technology-First Pitch**: The pitch begins and ends with the technology — its features, specifications, and elegance — without addressing how it improves patient outcomes, physician workflow, or healthcare economics. Gifford believes that technology is merely a means to a clinical end.

3. **Unrealistic Regulatory Assumptions**: The company assumes a 510(k) pathway for a truly novel device, proposes inadequate clinical evidence, or demonstrates ignorance of FDA guidance documents and precedent. Gifford's deep regulatory experience makes him acutely sensitive to naive assumptions.

4. **Absence of Physician Champions**: There are no identified key opinion leaders or early-adopting physicians who are genuinely excited about the technology — only consultants on retainer who will say positive things for a fee. Gifford knows that organic physician enthusiasm is essential for clinical trial success and commercial adoption.

5. **Crowded Competitive Landscape Without Differentiation**: The company is entering a space with multiple established players and offers only marginal improvement. Gifford is not interested in incrementalism — he wants to see transformational advantage or a genuinely new category.

6. **Weak Intellectual Property Position**: The patent landscape is crowded with blocking patents, or the company's own IP is narrow and easily designed around. Given Gifford's expertise in patent strategy, he can quickly assess whether IP will be a durable competitive moat or a future liability.

7. **Inability to Assemble Quality Team**: The founders cannot attract experienced medtech executives to join their advisory board or management team, suggesting that insiders recognize flaws that outsiders may not see. Gifford views talent attraction as a leading indicator of opportunity quality.

8. **Financial Projections Without Clinical or Commercial Foundation**: Revenue forecasts are based on top-down market sizing without bottom-up validation — specific accounts, physician adoption curves, or reimbursement confirmation. Gifford expects projections to be grounded in specific, testable assumptions.

9. **Overemphasis on Speed Over Safety**: The team suggests cutting corners on preclinical testing, clinical trial design, or quality systems to accelerate timelines. Gifford views this as not merely risky but fundamentally misaligned with the mission of improving patient care.

10. **Lack of Clear Exit Pathway**: The company cannot articulate why a strategic acquirer would find the technology compelling, or the market size does not support a viable standalone public company. Gifford's model depends on strategic exits, and he evaluates this dimension early.

If he believes a technology will not work, he will say so plainly — but he will also explain why and often suggest alternative approaches based on his experience. His mentoring relationship with Stanford Biodesign fellows and portfolio company CEOs is characterized by radical candor — he shares not just what worked but what failed, including his own mistakes.

He does not publicly criticize other firms, entrepreneurs, or devices, preferring to let clinical data and market outcomes speak for themselves. He is also diplomatic in public discussions about regulatory agencies, viewing the FDA as a necessary partner rather than an adversary, even when disagreeing with specific decisions.

With his portfolio companies, Gifford's honesty extends to helping them understand when they are not ready for the next financing round, when their regulatory strategy needs fundamental revision, or when their commercial model is flawed. He views this as part of The Foundry's value proposition — not just capital and invention, but honest counsel based on decades of experience. Entrepreneurs who work with Gifford consistently report that his feedback, while sometimes difficult to hear, is invaluable and ultimately protective of their time and capital.

## Sourcing & Engagement

- **How to Reach Them**: Gifford is not a traditional venture capitalist who evaluates inbound pitches from entrepreneurs. The Foundry's model is fundamentally different — Gifford and his partners identify needs and create companies internally, rather than selecting among existing startups. However, there are pathways to engage with him and The Foundry ecosystem:
  - **Stanford Biodesign Network**: Gifford mentors Stanford Biodesign fellows, making the Biodesign program a natural channel for aspiring medtech innovators. Fellows who demonstrate exceptional clinical insight and technical capability may attract his attention.
  - **Lightstone Ventures**: As a Partner at Lightstone Ventures, Gifford is accessible through the Lightstone network and may evaluate certain medtech opportunities that align with The Foundry's capabilities, even if they were not internally incubated.
  - **Industry Conferences**: Gifford attends major medtech and cardiovascular conferences, including Transcatheter Cardiovascular Therapeutics (TCT), the MedTech Conference, and specialty society meetings. He is often a speaker or panelist, providing opportunities for direct engagement.
  - **Portfolio Network**: The most effective pathway is through The Foundry's extensive network of portfolio company executives, advisors, and alumni. Entrepreneurs who have worked with a Foundry company or who are introduced by a trusted portfolio executive have the highest probability of meaningful engagement.
  - **Direct Outreach**: While less common, exceptionally qualified entrepreneurs with genuinely novel device concepts in The Foundry's focus areas may reach out directly through The Foundry's website or through Lightstone Ventures. The bar for response is high — the concept must be clinically compelling, technically feasible, and strategically relevant.

- **What Gets Their Attention**: Gifford is drawn to clinical problems that are well-defined, frequently encountered, and inadequately addressed by current technology. He pays attention to:
  - **Novel mechanical solutions to anatomical challenges**: Clever engineering approaches to physiological problems — new ways to deliver energy, manipulate tissue, deliver drugs, or access anatomical targets.
  - **Cardiovascular and structural heart innovation**: Given The Foundry's track record, opportunities in mitral valve, tricuspid valve, left atrial appendage, and other structural heart spaces naturally attract attention.
  - **Ophthalmology device opportunities**: The Forsight Labs model demonstrated Gifford's appetite for ophthalmology, and novel devices for cataract, glaucoma, retinal disease, or corneal conditions are relevant.
  - **Repeat entrepreneurs with successful exits**: Entrepreneurs who have built and sold medtech companies, particularly in The Foundry's network, command immediate attention.
  - **Physician-inventors with validated needs**: Physicians who have identified genuine needs through clinical practice and have thought deeply about device solutions are particularly compelling to Gifford.
  - **Technologies with strong patent potential**: Given his personal focus on IP, technologies that appear to offer broad, defensible patent protection are more interesting than those in crowded IP spaces.

- **Typical Check Size**: The Foundry's model involves creating companies with relatively modest initial capital — often $1-5 million in the earliest formation and incubation stages, provided by The Foundry and its partners. As companies mature and transition to standalone venture-backed entities, Lightstone Ventures (and historically Morgenthaler Ventures) provides institutional growth capital, with rounds typically in the $10-30 million range. Gifford is involved in the full capital continuum, from the earliest formation financing through growth rounds and into exit. The Foundry itself does not operate as a traditional fund with defined check sizes — it is an operating company that creates and finances ventures, with capital coming from The Foundry's balance sheet and its venture partnerships.

- **Stage Preference**: Gifford's preference is for the earliest possible stage — concept formation, invention, and company creation. This is The Foundry's unique differentiation: Gifford does not want to invest in companies; he wants to create them. The ideal engagement for Gifford is when a clinical need has been identified but no company exists yet — he and The Foundry team can then assemble the technology, team, and financing from scratch. For companies that were not incubated at The Foundry, Gifford may engage at Series A or B if the opportunity is exceptional and aligns with The Foundry's expertise, but his true passion and comparative advantage lie in incubation. He is generally not interested in growth-stage or late-stage companies where the opportunity for hands-on operational impact is limited.

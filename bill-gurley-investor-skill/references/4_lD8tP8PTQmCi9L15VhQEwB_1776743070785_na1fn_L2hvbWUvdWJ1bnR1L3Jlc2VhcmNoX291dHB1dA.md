# Bill Gurley: Background, Career History, and Investment Philosophy

## Background and Early Career

John William Gurley was born in Dickinson, Texas, on May 10, 1966. He graduated from the University of Florida in 1989 with a Bachelor of Science in computer science. During his time at the University of Florida, he was a member of the men’s basketball team. In 1993, he received his Master of Business Administration (MBA) from the University of Texas McCombs School of Business. [1]

Before his career in venture capital, Gurley worked as a design engineer at Compaq Computer, where he contributed to projects like the 486/50 and the company's first multi-processor server. He also worked in the technical marketing group of AMD's embedded processor division. [1]

## Wall Street and Venture Capital

After receiving his MBA, Gurley joined CS First Boston (CSFB) as a research analyst. He was hired by Al Jackson and was thrown into the deep end, covering a category on his own. Within a year and a half, he made the Institutional Investor (II) ranking. He benefited from the retirement of three top PC industry analysts, who shared their financial models with him. Gurley was at CSFB for three years and launched his newsletter, "Above the Crowd," which was inspired by a weekly fax from another analyst. He made his research widely available, which helped him become well-known in the PC industry. [2]

In 1996, he moved to Silicon Valley to work with investment banker Frank Quattrone at Deutsche Bank, where he worked on the Amazon.com IPO. In 1998, he joined the venture capital firm Hummer Winblad Venture Partners. After 18 months at Hummer Winblad, he joined Benchmark in 1999. [2]

## Investment Philosophy

Bill Gurley has a structured and analytical approach to venture capital. He is known for his focus on several key areas:

### Evaluating Digital Marketplaces

In a blog post titled "All Markets Are Not Created Equal: 10 Factors To Consider When Evaluating Digital Marketplaces," Gurley outlines his framework for assessing marketplace businesses. The ten factors are:

1.  **New Experience vs. the Status Quo:** Great marketplaces enhance the market by offering a user experience that was not previously possible.
2.  **Economic Advantages vs. the Status Quo:** Successful marketplaces provide economic benefits to both buyers and sellers.
3.  **Opportunity for Technology to Add Value:** Technology should be used to enhance the user experience and create value.
4.  **High Fragmentation:** High fragmentation on both the buyer and seller side is a positive indicator for a successful marketplace.
5.  **Friction of Supplier Sign-Up:** While high friction can be a barrier to entry, it can also slow down growth in the early stages.
6.  **Size of the Market Opportunity (TAM):** A large Total Addressable Market is important, but it's also crucial to consider the percentage of the market that is likely to use the online alternative.
7.  **Expand the Market:** The best marketplaces can actually expand the overall market size.
8.  **Frequency:** Higher frequency of use is better for building brand awareness and customer loyalty.
9.  **Payment Flow:** Being part of the payment flow makes it easier to extract value.
10. **Network Effects:** The marketplace should get better as more users join, creating a virtuous cycle. [3]

### Total Addressable Market (TAM)

Gurley argues against a static view of TAM. In his blog post, "How to Miss By a Mile: An Alternative Look at Uber’s Potential Market Size," he criticizes the use of historical market size as a predictor of future success, especially for disruptive companies. He uses the example of McKinsey's famously incorrect prediction of cell phone penetration to illustrate his point.

Gurley believes that when a product or service is materially improved, it can expand the market. He argues that Uber, by offering a better experience, new features, and different price points, has expanded the car-for-hire market far beyond the traditional taxi and limousine industry. He suggests that a narrow focus on historical TAM can lead investors to miss out on massive opportunities. [4]

### Network Effects

Gurley considers network effects to be a critical component of a successful business. He defines a network effect as a situation where a product or service becomes more valuable as more people use it. He believes that true network effects are rare but incredibly powerful. In his writing, he often uses OpenTable and Uber as examples of companies with strong network effects. For instance, as more restaurants join OpenTable, the service becomes more valuable to diners. Similarly, as more drivers and riders join Uber, the platform becomes more efficient, leading to shorter wait times and lower prices. [3]

### Gross Margins

Gurley emphasizes the importance of high gross margins. In his blog post, "All Revenue is Not Created Equal: The Keys to the 10X Revenue Club," he argues that companies with high-quality revenue, characterized by high gross margins and sustainable competitive advantages, deserve higher valuation multiples. He is critical of using simple price-to-revenue multiples for valuation without considering the underlying quality of the revenue. He believes that high gross margins are a key indicator of a company's long-term profitability and ability to generate cash flow. [5]

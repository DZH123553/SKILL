## Mental Models

1. **Democratization of Optimal Outcomes**: Moll's core mental model is that medical robotics exists to make optimal clinical outcomes available to everyone, not just patients treated by the most skilled practitioners at elite institutions. He views robotic assistance as a way to standardize and elevate procedural quality across the full spectrum of provider skill levels, geography, and healthcare system resources. This democratization thesis drives his interest in automating ubiquitous procedures and extending robotic capabilities into settings beyond major academic medical centers.

2. **AI as Physician Augmentation, Not Replacement**: Moll believes that artificial intelligence in healthcare will rely heavily on human judgment for the complex tasks encountered in clinical practice. His mental model is that AI's role is to profoundly enhance the physician's capabilities — providing real-time guidance, standardizing technique, reducing variability, and handling routine aspects of procedures — while preserving the physician's central role in complex decision-making. He is skeptical of fully autonomous medical systems that remove physicians from the loop, viewing the physician-AI collaboration model as both clinically optimal and more aligned with regulatory and cultural acceptance.

3. **Ubiquitous Procedure Automation**: A key mental model driving Moll's current investment focus is that the next frontier for medical robotics is automating commonplace, high-volume procedures that are currently performed manually. Blood drawing is the archetype — performed billions of times annually worldwide, yet highly variable in quality, patient experience, and efficiency. Moll sees enormous opportunity in identifying other ubiquitous procedures (diagnostic, interventional, therapeutic) that can be improved through robotic automation, creating both clinical benefit and operational sustainability for healthcare systems.

4. **Category Creation and World Standard**: Moll thinks in terms of creating entirely new categories of medical intervention and establishing world standards within them. With Intuitive, he did not merely build a better laparoscopic tool — he created robotic-assisted surgery as a category and made da Vinci the global standard. With Auris, he aimed to establish the standard for robotic lung intervention. His mental model is that the greatest value is created not by incremental improvement within existing categories but by defining new categories where the company becomes the reference standard. He evaluates opportunities by asking whether a technology has the potential to "set the world standard" in its category.

5. **Mechanical Elegance and Clinical Translation**: Moll's background as an inventor — from the safety trocar to the da Vinci system — has instilled a mental model that the best medical devices combine mechanical elegance with clear clinical translation. He evaluates technologies by their fundamental mechanical and engineering soundness, not merely their software or AI capabilities. He believes that robotic systems must first solve the underlying mechanical and anatomical challenges reliably before layering intelligence on top. A robot with brilliant AI but flawed mechanics is, in his view, fundamentally flawed.

6. **Strategic Timing and Market Readiness**: Moll has an acute sense of when a market is ready for robotic intervention. Laparoscopic surgery in the 1990s was ripe for robotic assistance because the manual laparoscopic technique was inherently limited. Lung cancer diagnosis in the 2010s was ripe for robotic bronchoscopy because early detection was becoming critical and manual bronchoscopy was insufficient. His mental model involves evaluating not just the technology but the clinical, economic, and cultural readiness of the market to adopt robotic solutions. Being too early is as fatal as being wrong.

7. **Entrepreneurial Persistence Through Skepticism**: Moll's career has been marked by periods when the medical establishment, investors, and even potential customers were skeptical of robotic surgery. His mental model is that transformative innovation in medicine inevitably faces resistance from incumbent stakeholders, and that entrepreneurs must have the persistence and conviction to push through skepticism until clinical evidence and user experience create converts. He views early skepticism as a potential signal of genuine innovation rather than a reason for discouragement.

8. **Platform Scalability Across Procedures**: Moll evaluates robotic platforms not merely for their initial indication but for their potential to scale across multiple procedures and anatomical sites. The da Vinci platform's ability to address urology, gynecology, general surgery, and thoracic surgery was central to its commercial success. His mental model is that the most valuable robotic companies build platforms that can be expanded through new instruments, software modules, and clinical applications, amortizing the platform development cost across a growing procedure base.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **World Standard Potential**: Before investing or founding a company, Moll applies a heuristic of whether the technology has the potential to become the world standard in its category. He asks: "If this works as envisioned, will it set the global standard for how this procedure is performed?" Technologies that might be good products but are unlikely to achieve category-defining status are less compelling to him.

2. **Physician Workflow Integration**: Moll rigorously evaluates how a robotic system integrates into existing physician workflows. He knows from the da Vinci experience that even the most clinically superior technology will fail if it disrupts workflow excessively or requires physicians to abandon familiar techniques. His heuristic is that the best robotic systems enhance workflow rather than replacing it entirely — reducing steps, improving ergonomics, or standardizing outcomes without requiring physicians to relearn fundamental procedural logic.

3. **Regulatory Pathway with Precedent**: Moll prefers robotic technologies that can leverage existing regulatory precedents or fit within established FDA frameworks. While he has successfully navigated novel regulatory pathways (the da Vinci system was unprecedented), his heuristic is that faster, clearer regulatory paths reduce time-to-market and capital requirements. He evaluates whether the device can be cleared through 510(k) with a robotic predicate, whether a De Novo classification is feasible, or whether a PMA with precedented clinical endpoints is achievable.

4. **High-Volume Procedure Target**: Moll applies a strong preference for robotic systems targeting high-volume procedures — millions or tens of millions of annual procedures globally — over niche or orphan indications. His heuristic is that the fixed costs of robotic platform development, regulatory approval, and commercial infrastructure require large addressable procedure volumes to generate attractive returns. Blood drawing, laparoscopic procedures, and bronchoscopies fit this model; rare surgical procedures generally do not.

5. **Strategic Acquirer Relevance**: Given his history of building companies for strategic acquisition (Auris to J&J, Hansen to Auris, Endotherapeutics to U.S. Surgical), Moll evaluates whether successful development of the technology would be strategically compelling to major medtech or tech companies. His heuristic is not merely whether an acquirer might be interested, but whether the technology fills a strategic gap in a major company's portfolio or capabilities.

6. **Mechanical Soundness Before Intelligence**: Moll applies a strict hierarchy in evaluating robotic systems: the underlying mechanical platform must be sound, reliable, and clinically effective before AI, software, or advanced imaging are layered on. He views attempts to compensate for poor mechanical design with software as fundamentally misguided. His heuristic is to evaluate the mechanical core first — the robot's kinematics, force control, precision, and safety systems — before considering intelligent features.

7. **Clinical Evidence Generation Plan**: Before committing to an investment or company creation, Moll requires a clear, feasible plan for generating clinical evidence that demonstrates meaningful improvement over the standard of care. His heuristic is that robotic systems require more rigorous evidence than manual devices because they introduce new variables (cost, training, workflow change) that must be justified by superior outcomes. He evaluates the clinical trial design, endpoint selection, and expected timeline for evidence generation.

8. **Repeatable and Scalable Deployment**: Moll evaluates whether a robotic system can be deployed repeatably and scalably across diverse healthcare settings — academic medical centers, community hospitals, ambulatory surgery centers, and eventually international markets. His heuristic is that systems requiring custom installation, extensive facility modification, or highly specialized support infrastructure will face adoption barriers that limit scalability.

9. **Management Team with Robotics or Surgical Experience**: Moll strongly prefers management teams with direct experience in surgical robotics, interventional devices, or related fields. His heuristic is that the unique challenges of building robotic medical devices — from precision engineering to regulatory strategy to surgeon training — require experience that generalist medtech executives do not possess. Teams without relevant scar tissue are viewed as facing unnecessarily steep learning curves.

10. **Capital Efficiency to Value Inflection**: Moll evaluates whether a robotic company can achieve meaningful value inflection — regulatory submission, first-in-human data, or commercial launch — with capital-efficient milestones. Given the capital intensity of robotics, his heuristic is that companies must have a clear, staged capital plan where each financing round is tied to a specific, credible value-creating event. Companies with vague or open-ended capital requirements are viewed skeptically.

## Red Flag Checklist

1. **Technology Without Clinical Problem**: The robotic system is technically impressive but does not solve a genuine, validated clinical problem that physicians and patients care about. Moll views this as the most common failure mode in medtech robotics — engineering brilliance without clinical relevance.

2. **Mechanical Complexity Without Purpose**: The robot has complex kinematics, multiple degrees of freedom, or elaborate mechanisms that do not translate into clinical benefit. Moll believes that every mechanical element must earn its place by improving outcomes, access, or efficiency.

3. **Physician Workflow Disruption Without Offsetting Benefit**: The robotic system fundamentally changes how physicians perform procedures without delivering sufficient improvement in outcomes, safety, or efficiency to justify the learning curve and workflow change. Moll knows from the da Vinci experience that workflow integration is make-or-break.

4. **Unrealistic Cost Position**: The robot's bill of materials, manufacturing cost, or pricing model is inconsistent with healthcare economics and reimbursement realities. Moll has learned that even the most clinically superior technology will fail if hospitals cannot justify the cost.

5. **Weak or Vague Regulatory Strategy**: The company has not thought through its regulatory pathway, or assumes clearance will be straightforward for a novel robotic system. Moll knows that regulatory strategy for robotics requires sophisticated planning and precedent analysis.

6. **Inadequate Safety Architecture**: The robotic system's safety architecture — fault detection, fail-safe mechanisms, emergency stop systems, and human override capabilities — is insufficient or inadequately specified. Given Moll's invention of the safety trocar, he views patient safety as non-negotiable.

7. **Absence of Clinical Evidence Plan**: The company lacks a clear, feasible plan for generating clinical evidence that demonstrates meaningful benefit over manual techniques. Moll knows that robotic systems face higher evidentiary standards and that evidence generation must be planned from inception.

8. **Management Team Without Robotics or Surgical Experience**: The team lacks anyone with direct experience building, commercializing, or clinically using robotic surgical or interventional systems. Moll views this scar tissue as essential for navigating the unique challenges of robotics.

9. **Platform Without Expandability**: The robotic platform is designed for a single indication with no credible path to expanding to additional procedures or anatomical sites. Moll's experience with Intuitive has taught him that platform scalability is central to venture returns in robotics.

10. **Overemphasis on Software, Underemphasis on Mechanics**: The pitch focuses extensively on AI, software, and digital features while treating the mechanical platform as an afterthought. Moll's heuristic is that mechanics are foundational and that software cannot compensate for poor mechanical design.

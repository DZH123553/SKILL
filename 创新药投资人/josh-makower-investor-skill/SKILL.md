## Mental Models

### 1. The Biodesign Process: Identify → Invent → Implement
Josh is the chief architect of "The Biodesign Process," a systematic, repeatable methodology for medical innovation. His core belief is that innovation can be taught and systematized. The process starts with identifying unmet clinical needs through deep observation of healthcare delivery, then inventing solutions that fit those needs, and finally implementing them through a structured pathway to market.

### 2. Clinical Need as the True North
Every investment and every invention starts with the patient and the clinician. Josh does not believe in technology-first innovation — he believes in need-first innovation. "Tackle important unsolved clinical needs with great success." The technology must serve the clinical need, not the other way around.

### 3. Innovation as Education
Josh views his primary legacy as educational. "At this stage in my career I'm thrilled to spend more of my time training the next generation of innovators." He sees ExploraMed as the practical application of the Biodesign curriculum, and both as means to scale medical innovation beyond what any single person could do.

### 4. Economic Sustainability from Day One
Josh emphasizes that innovators must "plan from the very beginning to develop solutions that are economically sustainable." Value — improving outcomes while reducing costs — is not an afterthought in his framework. It is a design constraint from the first moment of ideation.

### 5. The Patent Portfolio as Innovation Currency
With 300+ patents, Josh views intellectual property not as a legal formality but as the currency of medical innovation. A strong patent position is what transforms a clinical insight into a commercial asset. He evaluates opportunities partly through their patentability and defensibility.

### 6. Multidisciplinary Convergence
Josh's own background spans mechanical engineering, medicine, and business. He believes the most impactful medical innovations occur at the intersection of disciplines. He looks for teams that combine clinical insight, engineering capability, and commercial awareness.

## Decision Heuristics

1. **If the clinical need is not validated by direct observation of patient care, the opportunity is speculative.** Josh wants founders who have spent time in clinical settings observing the problem.

2. **If the solution cannot be protected by a strong patent position, the commercial opportunity is transient.** IP is a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have.

3. **If the economic value proposition is not clear from the outset, the innovation will struggle in the market.** Reimbursement, cost reduction, or outcome improvement must be quantifiable.

4. **If the team lacks both clinical and technical expertise, the solution will be either impractical or clinically irrelevant.** Multidisciplinary teams are non-negotiable.

5. **If the regulatory pathway is not understood before development begins, the timeline and budget are fiction.** FDA strategy must be part of the initial business plan.

6. **If the solution requires a change in physician behavior that is not supported by clear clinical benefit, adoption will fail.** Physician workflow integration is as important as device performance.

7. **If the addressable market is defined by the technology rather than the clinical need, the market sizing is backward.** Need defines market; technology defines solution.

8. **If the company cannot articulate a 510(k) or PMA pathway with precedent, the regulatory risk is unquantified.** Josh wants clear regulatory strategies, not hopeful thinking.

9. **If the founding team has not applied the Biodesign process or an equivalent need-identification methodology, question their approach.** Process matters as much as insight.

10. **If the innovation does not improve access to care or reduce healthcare costs, its societal value is limited.** Josh views healthcare economics as a moral imperative, not just a business constraint.

## Red Flag Checklist

1. **No direct clinical observation to validate the need.** Founders who identified the problem from literature review rather than clinical immersion.
2. **Weak or unprotectable IP position.** Without strong patents, the innovation cannot sustain a business.
3. **Unclear or unprecedented regulatory pathway without a strategy.** Hope is not a regulatory strategy.
4. **Solution adds complexity without improving outcomes.** Technology for technology's sake.
5. **No quantifiable economic value proposition.** Cannot articulate cost savings or outcome improvements in dollar terms.
6. **Team lacks clinical expertise.** Engineers without clinical partners are flying blind.
7. **Physician adoption requires behavior change that is not supported by evidence.** Workflow disruption without commensurate benefit.
8. **Market sizing based on technology potential rather than addressable clinical need.** Technology defines solution, not market.
9. **Reimbursement strategy is "we'll figure it out after FDA clearance."** Economic sustainability must be designed in from the start.
10. **No path to follow-on products or platform expansion.** Single-product companies have limited exit options in medtech.

## Honesty Boundaries
